SF Moma, Cooper Hewitt, Cube

Metagenomics

with Stamen Design, for UC Berkeley

We originally created this tool for researchers at UC Berkeley to study the genetics of micro-ecosystems in the gut of an infant, the inside of a nuclear reactor, or between a dolphin's teeth. Yet, it was also embraced by the art & design community: featured in SF Moma's Designed in California exhibit, and on the cover of the member calender, and then exhibited by the Cooper Hewitt, Smithsonian Design Museum in New York City and Cube Design museum in Kerkrade, Netherlands as part of the 2019 Design Triennial.

Interestingly, one of the most visually striking design decisions I made, the extreme contrast between the value 0 shown in black and the value 1 shown in near-white, was driven by the science. In most colorschemes, 0 and 1 are similar hues because 0 and 1 are numerically quite close. However, in this application, "none" and "at least 1" had completely opposite implications scientifically so they needed to look completely different.

Research: Visualizing Mix

InfoVis 2014, with Martin Wattenberg

Simpson's Paradox is a notoriously confusing effect in which aggregate numbers seem to contradict more detailed statistics. A "comet chart" is a special type of scatterplot which helps analysts detect and understand this type of situation. As a specific application, comet charts can reveal changes in voting patterns over time.

Data Visualization for Science, Analysis, & Decision Making

As a member of the Google Applied Science team I've contributed to research on drug discovery, materials science, automating insight from biological images, fusion, impoving scientific computing with machine learning, and more. While I sometimes consulted on scientific communication, my focus was not about communicating results. Rather, it was about working with the scientists during the discovery and analysis process: advising, prototyping, performing vis-based analysis, and building analysis tools. Success was defined as visualizations changing the scientific decisions (what model is chosen, what experiment to perform, change experimental conditions, etc), directly leading to a discovery, or using scientific data to inform a "real-world" decision.

Searching for New Malaria Drugs

Google Applied Science: Malaria

Over the course of three years, I worked closely with scientists and ML experts as they built models to create new methods in the effort to find new anti-malarial drugs.

Visualization played various critical roles throughout this work, from establishing a color scheme for a key concept used over the course of the project, to debugging the model, to interpreting the results. Being included as an author in the paper is a high bar, demonstrating that the work contributed not only to communicating results but also to the discoveries themselves.

Scientific Analysis Tool

Google Accelerated Science: Materials

The first version was a beautiful, interactive 3d surface. Unfortunately, it was useless for analyzing the data. This less "shiny" set of 48 lines chart shows 10x more data than the 3-d one, with each small chart represents a slice through the higher dimensional space. "User driven animation" allowed the researcher to engage more deeply with the data. Most importantly, this viz tool led to discovering an unexpected scientific phenomenon and played a key role in scientific decisions.

Viz for Scientific Communication

Google: Advising

While my primary role on the Applied Sciences team was creating visualizations for analysis & discovery, I also advised researchers across the team on how to more effectively communicate their results.

The "curse of knowledge" bias is especially challenging in transitioning from making a scientific discovery to communicating that same discovery: once you know what's important about the data in the chart, it's hard to imagine that someone else looking at the same chart wouldn't see the same thing.
