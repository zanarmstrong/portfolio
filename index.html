I am a data visualization engineer, designer, and researcher. As part of Google’s Applied Sciences team, I work with data analysts, scientists, and machine learning engineers to create visualizations that help them make discoveries.

Previously as a freelancer I created visualizations for Yale University, Kickstarter, Google, UC Berkeley, National Geographic, and more.

My work as a visualiztion specialist is grounded in my experience as a data scientist. I'm especially fascinated by crafting visualizations for analysis and decision-making where the discovery has not yet been made, but in which the creation process drives a conversation about what might be most important in the data and I create data-to-shape mappings that reveal those features.

SF Moma, Cooper Hewitt, Cube Design

Metagenomics

with Stamen Design, for UC Berkeley

We originally created this tool for researchers at UC Berkeley to study the genetics of micro-ecosystems in the gut of an infant, the inside of a nuclear reactor, or between a dolphin's teeth. Yet, it was also embraced by the art & design community: featured in SF Moma's Designed in California exhibit, and on the cover of the member calender, and then exhibited by the Cooper Hewitt, Smithsonian Design Museum in New York City and Cube Design museum in Kerkrade, Netherlands as part of the 2019 Design Triennial.

Interestingly one of the most visually striking design decisions I made, the extreme contrast between the value 0 shown in black and the value 1 shown in near-white, were driven by the science. In most colorschemes, 0 and 1 are similar hues because 0 and 1 are numerically quite close. However, in this application, "none" and "at least 1" had completely opposite implications scientifically so needed to look completely different.

Research: Visualizing Simpson's Paradox

InfoVis Paper 2014

This research shows how it's easy to draw the wrong causal conclusion using standard visualization methods when the sizes of subgroups of our data are changing over time. To address this, Martin Wattenberg and I introduced a variation on the scatterplot that reveals changes in mix: the "comet chart". In this chart, the metric of interest is on the y-axis and the size of each subgroup is on the x-axis. That way we can see changes in our metric, the size of the groups, or both. We can see if all groups are "flowing together" or if different groups are changing in different ways.

One specific application is to reveal changes in voting patterns between election years, with the number of voters on the x-axis and the % change for a particular party on the y-axis. More in this .

Research cited in Alberto Cairo’s The Truthful Art, Scott Murray's Interactive Data Visualization for the Web in addition to academic literature.

Scientific Analysis Tool

48 line charts with 10 lines each > a fancy 3-d one

This visualization resulted in the scientists discovering something about their experiment that they had assumed was not possible. Which led to my itereating on the visual form, creating the 'user-driven animated slope lines' in the bottom right, to make this feature of the data even more explorable.

One especially interesting aspect of this project was that the obvious visualization was to show it in 3-d as there were 3 key values of interest. Unfortunately, while the researchers cared most about changes in slope the inherent small-scale local noice in the experimental data made it too hard to see the bigger picture in the 3-d charts. The 48 lines charts actually showed 10x more data than the 3-d one, and were more effective for making new discoveries (publication in process).

Gene Expression in the Brain

Yale University

Researchers at Yale's Gerstein lab had an amazing dataset that they wanted to make more shareable and explorable for scientists. This dataset was particularly special because it included *quantitative* (rather than qualitative) measurements of how much genes in the brain were expressed and were specific to expression in different *cell types*. The design emphasized these features of the dataset uniquely valuable. A scatterplot shows both quantitative expression levels and fold enrichment, the dendrogram of nested cell types includes hover and click interactions to highlight/filter the scatterplot and table, and sortable table with a one-d plot reveals detailed expression measurements of each selected gene for all cell types.

Played a role in the analysis leading to this Nature Neurosience paper: A multiregional proteomic survey of the postnatal human brain

Perceptual uniformity *and* distance

What is the trade-off?

Perceptual uniformity, including when the colors are converted to greyscale, has become a highly valued attribute for data vis colorschemes.

Yet, there is a sacrifice: to achieve perceptual uniformity and other desired attributes, there is typically less overall perceptual differentiation. This means that values have to be further apart numerically to appear as noticably different colors. IE - if the numbers 1-100 were visualized using D3's "less angry rainbow" most people would be able to perceive the difference between 0 and 10, but those values would look the same using "viridis".

This set of charts explores the relationship between perceptual uniformity and perceptual distance for 11 common colormaps, and illustrates an alternative way to acheive uniformity without changing the hues by "straightening" the colormap.

Weather Circles (and Lines)

Mark Twain was wrong

I lost a bet. It is colder in January than June in San Francisco.

But, I made two visualizations showing typical hourly/daily cloudiness, temp, and windspeed based on 30 years of data from 25 cities in the US.

My favorite parts of this experiment was experiencing how different the same data felt when shown in these two different visual encodings. In one, each line is an hour of the day, x is month, and y is temp. In the other, each curve is one day of the year, the angle is the hour of day, and the distance from center is the temp.

Essay: "Why Choose: Scrollytelling & Steppers"

My Contributions to the Great Debate

In 2016, there was a great debate raging in the data visualization community: storytellers vs steppers. However, like the “which visualization is best” or the “are pie charts really evil” debates, these types of questions don’t really make sense to me.

The choices we make in data visualization and storytelling depend on so many things. It depends on your data. It depends on the crux of the meaning in the data and the story that you want to share. It depends on your audience. It depends on how you expect your audience to consume what you’ve created. It depends on your goal. It definitely depends on context. It depends on if it’s a one-off, or a tool that people will use again and again. And, it even depends on aesthetics and what the experience feels like. It just depends.
